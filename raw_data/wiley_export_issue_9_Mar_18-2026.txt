JO  - Acta Ophthalmologica
JA  - Acta Ophthalmol
VL  - 103
IS  - S284
SN  - 1755-375X
UR  - https://doi.org/10.1111/aos.16805
DO  - https://doi.org/10.1111/aos.16805

AB  - Retinal mechanisms evolved to extract and code efficiently the most important information present in retinal images. Luminance contrast, ?red/green?, ?yellow/blue?, ?white-black?, and ?light-flux? channels extract the signals needed to account for most attributes of our visual perception, including the ability to judge the level of ambient lighting. By employing two types of photoreceptors, rods and cones, the visual system achieves an extraordinarily large, dynamic range of useful vision, but this involves extensive compromises. The spatial, temporal and chromatic properties of the signals extracted from the retinal image very greatly with the level of ambient illumination. Visual acuity, contrast sensitivity, red/green and yellow/blue colour vision and motion and rapid flicker sensitivity, all worsen with decreasing light level. The extent to which visual performance is compromised in the mesopic range is also affected by normal aging and retinal disease. In this lecture I shall describe a number of advanced vision and optometric tests designed to reduce within subject variability, to account for normal age-related changes, to isolate selectively red/green and yellow/blue colour mechanisms and most importantly, to test specifically for the rate of loss of visual sensitivity in the mesopic range. Results from extensive investigations in patients with diabetes, age-related macular degeneration and glaucoma will be used to illustrate the usefulness of these tests in the early detection of retinal disease. Unique results from single patient studies will also be presented to illustrate some visual consequences of abnormal interaction of rod and cone signals in the mesopic range.

This comprehensive may offers deeper insights into ocular health and disease progression than conventional structural OCT. The synergy between Functional OCT and Deep Learning holds immense promise for the future of ocular imaging. By integrating these technologies, we can achieve a comprehensive understanding of ocular physiology and pathology while enhancing diagnostic accuracy and efficiency. This convergence may lead to the development of advanced systems capable of real-time analysis and interpretation, facilitating point-of-care diagnostics and remote monitoring. However, realizing this potential requires addressing various challenges, including data privacy concerns, standardization of imaging protocols, and regulatory approval. Collaborative efforts between researchers, clinicians, and industry stakeholders are essential to overcoming these hurdles and harnessing the full benefits of Functional OCT and Deep Learning in ophthalmic practice.

Ectasia of the cornea refers to diseases in which the radius of curvature and in parallel its refractive power changes and the cornea thins corresponding to the ectatic part. Ectasias are differentiated according to the form and the area of the cornea where the process occurs. The etiology of the disease is not known, and it used to be classified on clinical appearance. ECD is a heterogeneous disease, genetic and environmental factors may contribute to its pathogenesis. Ectasias are bilateral, progressive, non-inflammatory, rather sporadic, and familial occurrence has been found in only 6-20% of cases. The mode of inheritance can be autosomal dominant or recessive. Most common form is keratoconus, a progressive, non-inflammatory, usually bilateral, asymmetric disease, characterized by epithelial and stromal thinning and weakening. Pellucid marginal degeneration is characterized by thinning and protrusion of the cornea in the lower one-third. There are no signs of inflammation and no vascularization. It usually begins between 20 and 40?years of age. The cause of visual impairment is irregular astigmatism. Scarring occurs in the stroma near the posterior surface, but very rarely spontaneous perforation may occur. Terrien's marginal degeneration is a bilateral disease usually affecting men in their forties. However, significant asymmetry can also occur. In the upper part of the cornea, yellowish-white stromal opacities are visible 1-2?mm from the limbus, following its curve. Between the lesion and the limbus, the cornea has a normal structure. As the disease progresses, astigmatism and vascularization develop. The diagnosis of ECD is usually based on patient history and characteristic findings on topography and tomography, slit lamp signs often appear later, as the disease progresses. Typical signs on imaging include inferior steepening, skewing of radial axes, abnormal islands and elevations on anterior and posterior maps and abnormal corneal thinning. AB  - Screening for ectatic disease falls into two general categories; 1) refractive surgery screening, where the goal is to identify patients at potential risk for post-refractive ectasia, and 2) identifying patients with true ectatic disease (keratoconus) at the earliest possible point in the disease process to intervene to limit and/or prevent sequalae. Tomographic imaging (either Scheimpflug or OCT) allows for the earliest detection of disease by its ability to image all anterior segment surfaces. The Belin/Ambrosio Display (BAD) utilizes 9 different tomographic parameters in a proprietary regression formula and was designed to fulfill both functions (screening & diagnosis). The BAD display will show an elevated final ?D? in any corneal condition that varies from normal. Conditions such as corneal edema or post refractive surgery will produce an elevated final ?D? as these conditions differ from the normal cornea. Diagnosing keratoconus requires identifying those parameters that are specific for keratoconus that in addition to the final ?D?, raises the specificity for keratoconus. A current FDA Phase 3 protocol looks at posterior elevation at the thinnest point, and a Percent Thickness Increase (PTI) graph with the tracing below midline. These parameters added to a specified final ?D? would increase disease specificity. The BAD display has been shown to have both high sensitivity and specificity, but screening values can be adjusted to suit the clinical or study situation.

TI  - Why have we failed to fully explain incomplete penetrance in lhon?
JO  - Acta Ophthalmologica
JA  - Acta Ophthalmol
VL  - 103
IS  - S284
SN  - 1755-375X
UR  - https://doi.org/10.1111/aos.16816
DO  - https://doi.org/10.1111/aos.16816
PY  - 2025
AB  - Leber hereditary optic neuropathy (LHON) remains puzzling in many aspects, one of these is the incomplete penetrance. Three mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) point mutations affecting genes that encode for complex I subunits are associated with LHON in over 90% of cases. As mtDNA variants may co-exist with wild type genomes, a condition known as ?heteroplasmy?, the disease expression may need a high threshold of mutant load. However, heteroplasmy occurs occasionally in LHON families, as in most cases the mtDNA primary LHON mutation is found to be homoplasmic, i.e. 100% mutant mtDNA, but yet the affected individuals remain only a limited subset of the mutation carriers. Variation of the mitochondrial genome has been also studied as possible source of incomplete penetrance. In fact, constitutive variants defining mtDNA haplogroup J have been found to increase penetrance in families with the m.11778G>A and, more consistently, with the m.14484T>C mutations. Yet this observation does not completely explain incomplete penetrance. Remarkably, incomplete penetrance is also found to occur with the new entity of recessive LHON, in families with DNAJC30 mutations. Environmental factors, such as tobacco smoking and alcohol consumption, are also invoked as disease triggers able to impact on penetrance. Last, nuclear DNA (nDNA) variants have been intensively investigated, but with rare exceptions, to date no common polymorphic or hypomorphic variants have been found to segregate with affected or unaffected mutation carriers, thus modulating penetrance. Furthermore, no clearcut digenic model with variants in chromosome X, as initially hypothesized to explain also male prevalence, have been convincingly documented. It seems obvious that all models hypothesized until now did not work and a paradigm shift seems necessary. Multi-omic approaches are ongoing to further investigated the unsolved issue of incomplete penetrance in LHON, under the assumption that variants burden in multiple genes belonging to specific pathways related to LHON pathogenic mechanisms may be key to modulate penetrance. One of these pathways is hypothesized to be the machinery regulating mitochondrial biogenesis and mtDNA copy number, as there is evidence that successfully upregulated mito-biogenesis is a compensatory mechanism characterizing unaffected mutation carriers as compared to affected and controls. Other candidate pathways may include the whole machinery necessary for complex I biogenesis and maintenance, as recently shown for the digenic cases of Leigh syndrome occurring with the m.11778G>A or DNAJC30 mutations. Furthermore, the genetics underlying some anatomical traits that may be relevant to favoring disease conversion, such as optic nerve head dimension, may be other good candidates to screen. Epigenetics remains unexplored, as well as the confounder of environmentally driven affected cases may reduce statistical power in identifying relevant genetic modifying variants. Overall, it is necessary to join new strategies and hypothesis with a common international multicentric effort to build a large LHON cohort to convincingly solve the controversial issue of incomplete penetrance in LHON.

AB  - Introduction: Cancer-associated retinopathy (CAR) represents a paraneoplastic syndrome characterized by profound visual impairment associated with autoantibodies targeting retinal antigens. Case report: Herein, we present a clinical case of CAR in a 66-year-old male tobacco user diagnosed with microcytic lung cancer, concurrently exhibiting very high titers of antirecoverin antibodies. The patient's ocular symptoms manifested as bilateral vision loss and photopsias, prompting comprehensive ophthalmological evaluation. The patient's medical history revealed treatment with atezolizumab, a monoclonal antibody targeting programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1), as part of his anticancer regimen. Notably, atezolizumab is known for its immunomodulatory effects, which have shown efficacy in various malignancies. However, recent literature suggests a potential association between immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) and the exacerbation of autoimmune phenomena, including CAR. Ophthalmologic examination revealed loss of extrafoveal ellipsoid layer in OCT, bilateral diffuse retinal pigmentary changes with attenuated arterioles and electroretinogram (ERG) findings consistent with severe dysfunction of both rod and cone photoreceptors, indicative of advanced retinal degeneration. This case highlights the importance of heightened vigilance for ocular adverse events in patients receiving ICIs, particularly those with pre-existing autoimmune tendencies. Prompt recognition and management of CAR are paramount to mitigating irreversible visual impairment and improving patient outcomes. Future studies elucidating the interplay between ICIs and retinal autoimmunity are warranted to optimize therapeutic strategies and minimize iatrogenic complications. Conclusions: In conclusion, we present a compelling case of CAR in a patient with microcytic lung cancer treated with atezolizumab, suggesting a potential association between ICI therapy and exacerbation of immune-mediated retinal damage. Clinicians should maintain a high index of suspicion for ocular complications in patients receiving ICIs, and further research is needed to delineate the underlying mechanisms and optimize management strategies in this population.

AB  - A 75-year-old Caucasian male, with a medical history of hypertension, diabetes, and dyslipidemia, presented to the emergency department with sudden complete vision loss in his left eye (LE). Ophthalmological examination revealed absence of light perception, a relative afferent pupil defect, and mild nuclear cataract with normal intraocular pressure. Fundoscopy indicated optic disc edema, retinal arteriolar narrowing, venous tortuosity, macular whitening, a foveal cherry-red spot, and scattered hemorrhages in the peripheral retina and vitreous turbidity. Right eye (RE) had a best corrected visual acuity (BCVA) of 20/30, with mild nuclear cataract as the only anomaly. Spectral domain optical coherence tomography (SD-OCT) of the RE displayed hyperreflective inner retinal layers, intra- and subretinal fluid, hyperreflective vitreous structure, and irregular preretinal formations. Fluorescein angiography indicated retinal non-perfusion, suggesting combined central retinal artery and vein occlusion (CCRAVO). Fast-track protocol for excluding Giant Cell Arteritis was initiated, followed by extensive assessment for non-arteritic central retinal artery occlusion, which yielded unremarkable results except for preexisting medical conditions and risk factors. Two weeks later, the patient returned with reduced visual acuity in RE. BCVA was 20/40 with fundoscopy revealing creamy focal opacities at posterior pole and mid-periphery, and vitreous haze. SD-OCT of lesions showed full-thickness hyperreflectivity and disruption of retinal layers. LE exhibited mild anterior uveitis, inflammatory vitreous cells, and necrotic areas in peripheral and mid-peripheral retina. Bilateral acute retinal necrosis (ARN) was suspected, confirmed via polymerase chain reaction (PCR) analysis of aqueous humor from LE, detecting varicella-zoster virus (VZV). Hospitalized, the patient received intravenous acyclovir and intravitreal foscarnet for new peripheral lesion in RE. Oral glucocorticoids were introduced. Oral acyclovir and gradual tapering of glucocorticoids continued. Nine months post-onset, RE BCVA improved to 20/30, and patient received prophylactic oral acyclovir. Isolated CCRAVO, though rare, may suggest VZV occlusive vasculitis. ARN's exact mechanism remains uncertain; Kyrieleis vasculitis suggests immune-mediated process. (1,2) Comorbidities may predispose to central retinal vascular events within ARN. Comprehensive ophthalmological follow-up is crucial, especially with early features like vitreous involvement and inflammatory precipitates. (3,4) Increased awareness of ARN's potential sight-threatening effects on ocular vasculature aids timely identification. AB  - Tear fluid is a complex mixture of proteins, lipids, and other molecules essential for maintaining ocular surface health. In recent years, tear fluid has emerged as an invaluable source of biomarkers, reflecting not only ocular health, but also providing glimpses into systemic and neurological well-being. Tears can be used to gather abundant molecular information that can be harnessed for diagnostic, monitoring, predictive, and prognostic purposes. The unique tear fluid molecular profile of each patient facilitates tailored interventions, enhancing treatment efficacy and minimizing adverse effects. Besides being continuously accessible, tear fluid can be sampled in a non-invasive and inexpensive way. This makes the sampling process patient friendly, thereby enhancing patient compliance and reducing the risk of potential complications associated with invasive sampling techniques. The non-invasive nature of tear fluid sampling makes it suitable for repeated measurements over time, allowing for longitudinal studies and monitoring of dynamic changes in biomarkers. Additionally, tear fluid requires minimal storage requirements, as it can be collected and stored using simple and readily available equipment. To date, hundreds of tear biomarkers have been discovered, but only a few have made it to the clinic. Major progress in translating newly discovered tear biomarkers into diagnostic tests is still missing because of the unique challenges that have yet to be addressed. Variability in tear fluid results poses a significant challenge in ocular research due to the lack of standardized methods for collection, analysis, interpretation, and reporting. This variation makes it difficult to draw consistent conclusions across different studies. Inconsistent research findings result from differences in methodologies and small sample sizes, leading to conflicting reports on disease-specific tear proteins and outcomes. Moreover, the lack of standardization hampers our understanding of tear fluid physiology and its role in diseases, impeding the development of diagnostic tools and therapies. This lack of consensus also hinders the clinical translation of tear-based diagnostics and therapies, potentially delaying accurate diagnoses and appropriate treatments for patients. Recognizing the importance of methodological standardization, various initiatives have been proposed to address these challenges. Collaborative efforts involving researchers, clinicians, industry partners, and regulatory agencies aim to establish consensus guidelines for sample collection, processing, and analysis. These guidelines emphasize the importance of rigorous validation studies, quality control measures, and transparent reporting practices to enhance the reliability and reproducibility of tear fluid biomarker research. Through collaborative efforts and adherence to consensus guidelines, the field can realize the full potential of tear fluid biomarkers in revolutionizing the diagnosis, monitoring, and treatment of ocular diseases.

To date only tests to measure MMP-9 in the tear film, and bed side osmometers to test tear film osmolarity are available for daily patient care. The MMP-9 point-of-care test is primarily a qualitative dichotomous test that stains positive if the value is above 40 ng/ml. Their problems in daily care of ocular surface disease patients and developments for the future will be discussed.
